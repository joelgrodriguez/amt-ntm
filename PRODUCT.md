# Product

brand

## Users

The site speaks to working contractors first, with distributors and larger metal-roofing companies reading over their shoulder.

- **Primary: independent contractor and crew-owner.** Owns and runs a roofing, gutter, or sheet-metal crew. Buying or shopping an NTM machine is a five- to six-figure decision they make personally, often after years of subcontracting the rollforming work and watching the margin walk out the door. ROI-driven, skeptical of marketing, more likely to trust a peer's video than a brochure. Often 40+, frequently 50+. Uses the site from a phone in a truck, a shop laptop, or a desktop after hours.
- **Secondary: estimator or owner at an established roofing / metal panel company.** Bigger purchase committee. They land on the about page to vet legitimacy before sending the link to the boss.
- **Tertiary: international distributor or dealer.** Different conversation: reliability, parts pipeline, training, brand prestige. NTM has shipped machines to 40+ countries; the about page has to read credibly to someone in Ghana or Russia, not just Aurora.

Across all three, the job-to-be-done on the about page is the same: *quickly verify that NTM is the real thing*, the company you'd bet a major capital purchase on, and that the machines on the other pages are backed by a serious operation.

## Product Purpose

newtech-ntm.com is the marketing site for **New Tech Machinery (NTM)**, the world's leading manufacturer of portable rollforming machines (metal roof panel machines, seamless gutter machines, and the control systems that run them). NTM invented the modern portable roof panel category with the SSP machine in 1991 and the modern portable seamless gutter category with the MACH II in 1994. Headquartered in Aurora, Colorado; second manufacturing facility in Hermosillo, Mexico (2004); part of Mazzella Companies since 2015; machines deployed in 40+ countries.

The site exists to convert serious shoppers into specialist conversations: configurator sessions, quote requests, and dealer/distributor calls. It is not a transactional storefront. The job of every surface is to reduce the buyer's perceived risk and make the next conversation easier.

The about page in particular has to do three things at once: (1) plant the leadership flag (we invented this, we're still the standard), (2) show longevity and stability (34+ years, second plant, Mazzella backing, 40+ country reach), and (3) read like the company that built the machines on every other page. Aesthetic coherence with the machine pages is part of the proof.

## Brand Personality

**Pioneering. Grounded. Precision-built.**

- **Pioneering, not pioneering-in-the-past.** NTM invented this category and is still setting the standard. Leadership is current tense. Avoid heritage-brand nostalgia ("since 1991" as a sentimental flourish); 1991 is the receipt for a claim we still make today, not a memory.
- **Grounded, not rugged.** American-manufactured, durable, multi-generation contractor trust shows up in copy and proof points. It does NOT show up as mud, sparks, sepia, or distressed textures. The brand is industrial-premium, not industrial-rugged. The Cybertruck-not-the-Wrangler instinct.
- **Precision-built.** The aesthetic of an engineered spec sheet, presented with the restraint of a premium product brand. The machines are six-figure precision instruments; the site has to look like one too. Zero ornament, mono-editorial labeling, hairline-blueprint structure, full-bleed product hero photography.

Voice in copy: plainspoken, opinionated, confident without bragging. Sentences a working contractor would actually say. Never corporate, never sentimental, never overpolished. When we make a leadership claim ("the world's finest portable rollforming machines"), we make it as a statement of fact, not as a slogan.

## Anti-references

What this site explicitly is not, in order of how often the trap shows up in this category:

- **Corporate handshake / B2B stock photography.** No hardhats-shaking-hands, no boardroom shots, no smiling-diverse-team-around-a-laptop. The generic "Our Story" visual grammar is a near-instant credibility loss for this buyer.
- **Rugged dirt-and-sparks industrial cliches.** No mud, sparks, grime, harsh orange light, gloved hands gripping steel. The category defaults to this; we explicitly do not. Industrial-premium beats industrial-rugged for the buyer we want.
- **Generic SaaS "About" template.** No big-number metric hero with gradient accents, no identical team-member card grid, no three-icon centered-stack value props. If a B2B SaaS company could ship this page unchanged, restart.
- **Heritage-brand sepia nostalgia.** No vintage type, no faux-letterpress, no "Since 1991" as a craft-brand sentiment. We are 34 years old, not 134, and we sell engineered equipment, not artisan furniture.
- **Mazzella-as-the-headline.** The Mazzella acquisition (2015) is a stability proof point, not the page's protagonist. NTM is the brand the buyer is here for.

## Design Principles

Strategic principles, not visual rules. The visual rules live in DESIGN.md.

1. **The machine is the brag.** Lead with the equipment, the firsts, the country count, the years. Never lead with mission statements, values posters, or "we believe" framing. Show what was built; let the buyer assemble the conclusion.
2. **Engineered, not corporate.** Every surface should read like it was made by the people who build the machines, not by a marketing department. If a paragraph could appear unchanged on a generic B2B site in any category, rewrite it.
3. **Leadership as fact, not slogan.** When we say we're the industry standard, we back it with the dated proof: SSP 1991, MACH II 1994, polyurethane drive roller original-equipment claim, 40+ countries, 10+ category firsts. The receipts carry the boast.
4. **Aesthetic coherence is a proof point.** A buyer scanning the about page is also vetting whether this is the same company that built the machine pages. The about page must use the same visual grammar (chrome bars, hairline blueprint, mono-editorial labeling) as the rest of the site. Drift between marketing pages reads as institutional sloppiness.
5. **Restraint scales; ornament doesn't.** The brand is 34 years old and shipping to 40+ countries; that scale is communicated through discipline (one accent color, two fonts, zero radius, no shadows) more than through volume. Quiet pages read older and more trusted than busy ones.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA across the site, with no further public commitments. Practical implications for the about page and the wider marketing surfaces:

- Body copy minimum 16px, no exception. Buyers skew 50+ and often read on phones in sunlight.
- Color contrast meets AA at minimum; aim higher on dark-section body copy where the blue-200-on-blue-900 combination sits close to the line.
- Tap targets generous on mobile; CTAs are not 32px buttons.
- `prefers-reduced-motion` is enforced, not optional (this is already in DESIGN.md §9; reaffirming here).
- Real heading hierarchy and landmark structure on every page, including marketing surfaces. The about page is one `<h1>`, then `<h2>` per section.
- Video sections (Who Is NTM and others) include captions or transcripts where the video carries content load.
